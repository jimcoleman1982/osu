Future HOF Players

Doug, you picked one of the best possible seven-year windows to be at Oklahoma State. When you arrived in 1982, Jimmy Johnson was the head coach. When you graduated in 1988, Barry Sanders had just put up the greatest individual season in college football history and won the Heisman Trophy. In between, you watched two future Pro Football Hall of Famers line up in the same backfield.

The Coaches

From Jimmy Johnson to Pat Jones — two men who elevated the program

Jimmy Johnson

Head Coach 1979–1983 • Record: 29–25–3

Johnson stabilized the Cowboys program and took them to two bowl games. He was named Big 8 Coach of the Year in his first season after posting a 7–4 record. His final season in 1983 ended with a Bluebonnet Bowl victory over Baylor, 24–14.

Then he left for Miami — where he'd win a national championship — before moving to the Dallas Cowboys, where he won two Super Bowls. Doug, Jimmy Johnson was your coach as a freshman and sophomore.

Pat Jones

Head Coach 1984–1994 • Record at OSU: 62–60–3

Jones had served five years as Johnson's assistant before taking over. In his very first season, the Cowboys went 10–2 — the school's first 10-win season in history. He was named Big 8 Coach of the Year.

He would post three 10-win seasons in his first five years (1984, 1987, 1988), overseeing what was described as "the most successful period in school history at the time."

Barry Sanders — 1988

The greatest individual season in college football history. Doug's graduation year.

Doug, you graduated the same year this happened. This is your Heisman.

In 1988, Barry Sanders did something that had never been done and hasn't been done since. As a junior, after spending two years as Thurman Thomas's backup, Sanders exploded for 2,628 rushing yards and 37 touchdowns in just 11 regular-season games. He set 34 NCAA records. He scored at least two touchdowns in every single game. He had five consecutive 200-yard games.

The Cowboys posted 40+ points in each of their first seven games. Sanders averaged 8.3 yards per carry. He produced 3,250 all-purpose yards. It wasn't just the best season of the year — it was the best season anyone had ever seen.

The Heisman

Sanders won the Heisman Trophy in a landslide, receiving 559 first-place votes compared to 70 for USC's Rodney Peete and 31 for UCLA's Troy Aikman. He swept all six voting regions.

The twist: Sanders learned he'd won the Heisman via satellite from Tokyo, Japan, where the team was preparing to face Texas Tech in the Coca-Cola Classic.

The Holiday Bowl Finale

As if the regular season wasn't enough, Sanders added 222 rushing yards and 5 touchdowns in the Holiday Bowl against Wyoming (Oklahoma State 62, Wyoming 14). His final season totals: 2,850 rushing yards, 42 rushing touchdowns, 44 total touchdowns.

He was selected 3rd overall in the 1989 NFL Draft by the Detroit Lions. He's been inducted into both the College Football Hall of Fame and the Pro Football Hall of Fame.

Thurman Thomas

OSU's all-time leading rusher — and the man Sanders backed up

Here's a fact that still blows minds: Barry Sanders spent two years as Thurman Thomas's backup. The only time in college football history that two future Pro Football Hall of Fame running backs played on the same team — and one was sitting on the bench.

Thomas was the starter from 1984 to 1987. He finished as Oklahoma State's all-time leading rusher with 4,847 career yards and 43 touchdowns. He was a two-time All-American (1985 and 1987), with 1,600+ yards and 15+ touchdowns in both of those seasons.

Drafted in the second round by the Buffalo Bills in 1988, Thomas went on to a 13-year NFL career with five Pro Bowl selections. His jersey is retired at Oklahoma State.

The Supporting Cast

It wasn't just Sanders and Thomas

Hart Lee Dykes

Wide Receiver • 1985–1988

224 receptions, 3,510 yards, 31 TDs. Consensus All-American in 1988. Drafted #16 overall by the New England Patriots.

Leslie O'Neal

Defensive End • 1981–1984

OSU's all-time sack leader with 34 career sacks. 16 sacks in 1984 alone (still the school record). Drafted #8 overall. Six-time NFL Pro Bowler. College Football Hall of Fame (2020).

Mike Gundy

Quarterback • 1986–1989

Became the all-time leading passer in Oklahoma State and Big 8 Conference history. Threw the ball to Dykes and handed it off to Sanders. Later became OSU's head coach.

Rusty Hilger

Quarterback • 1981–1984

Quarterbacked the 1984 team to OSU's first-ever 10-win season. Set school records for completions, attempts, and yards. Drafted by the LA Raiders. Seven-year NFL career.

Bedlam Heartbreakers

0–7 against Oklahoma — but every game has a story

The Cowboys went 20–0 against everyone not named Oklahoma or Nebraska in 1987–88. But against OU? The Sooners won every Bedlam game during Doug's years. Some of them still sting.

1983
Oklahoma 21, Oklahoma State 20. One point. That's all that separated them.
1984
#2 Oklahoma 24, #3 Oklahoma State 14. Both teams ranked in the top 3. One of the biggest Bedlam games ever played.
1985
"The Ice Bowl." Oklahoma 13, Oklahoma State 0. Subzero wind chill at Lewis Field. OU went on to win the national championship that year.
1988
Oklahoma 31, Oklahoma State 28. OSU's first-ever national telecast on ESPN. Barry Sanders rushed for 215 yards. The whole country was watching. This game essentially clinched Sanders' Heisman Trophy.

Lewis Field

Home of Cowboys football during Doug's years

During the 1980s, Oklahoma State played at Lewis Field (later renamed Boone Pickens Stadium) with a capacity of approximately 40,000. The stadium saw several upgrades during Doug's time:

  • 1985: Night football played for the first time under the lights
  • 1987: New AstroTurf surface installed (cost: $400,000+)
  • 1979 record: All-time attendance of 51,458 for a Bedlam game — set just before Doug arrived
