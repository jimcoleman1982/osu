The Prodigal Son

Eddie Sutton

He played under Iba. He came home. He changed everything.

Edward Eugene Sutton

The Prodigal Son

March 12, 1936 — May 23, 2020 • Bucklin, Kansas

Eddie Sutton was born in the tiny town of Bucklin, Kansas — 130 miles west of Wichita. His father worked as a mechanic, electrician, and plumber. He enrolled at Oklahoma A&M and played guard under the legendary Hank Iba from 1955 to 1958. He called Iba his "second father." Then he spent the next four decades becoming one of the most successful coaches in college basketball history — 806 wins, four schools in the NCAA Tournament, and the Naismith Basketball Hall of Fame.

"Always have faith in God, Yourself and the Cowboys." — Eddie Sutton

Building a Coaching Career

From Bucklin to Stillwater — and everywhere in between

After playing for Iba and serving as his graduate assistant in 1958–59, Sutton set out on a coaching journey that would span nearly four decades and five programs before he came home.

Tulsa Central High School

1959–1966
119–51

Four state tournament appearances. Named Coach of the Year at city, conference, state, and national levels. The Iba defensive system worked at every level.

College of Southern Idaho

1966–1969
83–14

Sutton became the school's first-ever head basketball coach. In three seasons, he won 83 games and lost just 14.

Creighton University

1969–1974
Five consecutive winning seasons

Inherited a program with three straight losing seasons and immediately turned it around. Culminated in a 23–7 season and NCAA Tournament appearance in 1973–74.

University of Arkansas

1974–1985
260–75 (.776)

Eleven seasons of dominance. Five Southwest Conference championships. Nine NCAA Tournament appearances. 1978 Final Four with "The Triplets" — Ron Brewer, Marvin Delph, and Sidney Moncrief. His .776 winning percentage remains the highest in SWC history.

University of Kentucky

1985–1989
88–39

32–4 his first season, named National Coach of the Year. But the tenure ended after four seasons following recruiting violations and sanctions. Sutton and his entire staff were forced to resign.

Coming Home: The OSU Transformation

1990–2006 — From afterthought to powerhouse

On April 11, 1990, Eddie Sutton returned to his alma mater. The program he inherited was in ruins: one NCAA Tournament appearance in 25 years. Only seven winning seasons in that same stretch. Oklahoma State basketball was an afterthought.

Sutton changed that immediately.

Year One: Instant Impact

In Sutton's first season (1990–91), the Cowboys went 24–8, won the Big 8 regular season championship, and advanced to the NCAA's Sweet 16. Henry Iba — watching from Stillwater — couldn't have been happier.

The Three D's

Sutton's core principles were printed on the players' practice jerseys and shorts: Discipline, Dedication, and Defense. His teams excelled through a disciplined, defense-oriented style emphasizing rebounding and quick transition play, pressing to force turnovers and capitalize on fast breaks.

As assistant Charlie Spoonhour described it: "The deal was not just guarding your man, it was denial of passes, playing the passing lanes. It was perfected by the inch."

1995 Final Four

Big Country, the broken backboard, and a return to the national stage

In Sutton's fifth season, the Cowboys rode the power of 7-foot center Bryant "Big Country" Reeves and sharpshooter Randy Rutherford to the school's first Final Four since 1951.

The Backboard Moment

During Final Four practice in Seattle on March 31, 1995, Big Country Reeves threw down a dunk that shattered the Kingdome backboard. Glass everywhere. The entire country saw it. After that, everyone knew who Bryant Reeves was.

Oklahoma State advanced by beating #2 seed Massachusetts 68–54 in the East Region final. Reeves played all 40 minutes, posting 24 points and 10 rebounds, and was named the regional's most outstanding player.

The Cowboys lost 74–61 to eventual national champion UCLA on April 1, 1995. But the statement had been made: Oklahoma State basketball was back.

2004 Final Four

The one Doug remembers — March 27, 2004

Doug, you mentioned this one by name. March 27, 2004. Here's what happened.

The 2003–04 Cowboys were Sutton's masterpiece: a 31–4 team that tied the school record for wins and swept the Big 12 regular season and tournament titles. The roster was built on an unlikely collection of transfers — five from other four-year schools, two from junior colleges — who came together into something special.

The Roster

Tony Allen JC transfer. Leading scorer. Future 6x NBA All-Defensive player. Kobe Bryant's toughest defender.
John Lucas III Floor general. Alongside Allen, led the backcourt.
Joey Graham Key contributor. Future NBA Draft pick.
Stevie Graham Joey's brother. Transfer from Central Florida.
Daniel Bobik Transfer from BYU.
Ivan McFarlin Power forward. 25th overall pick in 2004 NBA Draft.

The Road to San Antonio

On March 27, 2004, Oklahoma State defeated St. Joseph's in the Elite Eight to punch their ticket to the Final Four at the Alamodome in San Antonio.

In the national semifinal, the Cowboys faced Georgia Tech. It came down to the wire — Georgia Tech 67, Oklahoma State 65. Two points from the championship game.

It remains the last time Oklahoma State has been to a Final Four.

His Legacy

806 wins, the Hall of Fame, and a court that bears his name

Eddie Sutton Court

On January 15, 2005, the court at Gallagher-Iba Arena was officially renamed Eddie Sutton Court. The floor where Hank Iba built a dynasty now carries the name of the student who came back and built another one.

Hall of Fame: Class of 2020

After being a finalist seven times since 2002, Eddie Sutton was finally elected to the Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame on April 4, 2020. The announcement came on ESPN. He was 84 years old.

He was inducted alongside Kobe Bryant, Tim Duncan, Kevin Garnett, and Tamika Catchings — one of the greatest classes in Hall of Fame history.

May 23, 2020

Eddie Sutton died of natural causes on May 23, 2020, at age 84. He was surrounded by family. Former players and close friends had visited in the days before his passing.

He died less than two months after learning he would be inducted into the Hall of Fame. He was enshrined posthumously alongside Kobe Bryant on August 29, 2020.

"Leadership, like coaching, is fighting for the hearts and souls of men and getting them to believe in you." — Eddie Sutton

Tony Allen, who played on the 2004 Final Four team, credited Sutton with molding him into an NBA champion and the toughest defender Kobe Bryant ever faced.

Oklahoma State president Burns Hargis said: "He revived our historic basketball program and will always be revered and loved by the Cowboy family."
