The House of Horrors — Gallagher-Iba Arena, 1982–1988

Gallagher-Iba Arena

The House of Horrors — where visiting teams came to suffer

If you watched Cowboys basketball in the 1980s, you experienced something most arenas couldn't replicate. Gallagher-Iba Arena was a pressure cooker — literally. Until the 1987 renovation, the building had no air conditioning. The heat from thousands of fans packed into steep, 34-degree-angle seating created a suffocating wall of orange that terrorized visiting teams for decades.

The arena earned its fearsome nickname during the Henry Iba era, when the combination of stifling defense on the court and stifling conditions in the stands made it one of the most hostile environments in college basketball. Opponents didn't just lose games there — they wilted.

In 1987, the building underwent a major renovation. Air conditioning was finally installed, and the arena was officially renamed Gallagher-Iba Arena — honoring both wrestling coach Ed Gallagher and the legendary Hank Iba. Doug, you were there when that happened.

Arena Fast Facts

Opened: 1938

Renamed: 1987 (while Doug was a student)

Named for: Ed Gallagher (wrestling) & Henry Iba (basketball)

Seating angle: 34 degrees — one of the steepest in college basketball

The floor: Original white maple — the oldest basketball court still in use

Court name: Eddie Sutton Court (dedicated 2005)

Nickname: "The House of Horrors"

No AC until: 1987 renovation

The Breakthrough: 1982–83

Doug's sophomore year — the best season in a generation

The 1982–83 Cowboys were special. Under coach Paul Hansen, Oklahoma State went 24–7 — Hansen's best season and one of the program's finest in decades.

The signature moment came in the Big 8 Conference Tournament championship, where the Cowboys defeated Missouri in double overtime. That victory earned OSU the automatic bid to the NCAA Tournament — their first appearance since 1965, ending an 18-year drought.

The tournament run ended in heartbreak: a first-round upset loss to #12 Princeton, 56–53, at Gill Coliseum in Corvallis, Oregon. But making it there at all was a statement — Oklahoma State basketball was alive again.

The Coaches Doug Watched

Two very different eras during your time in Stillwater

Paul Hansen

Head Coach 1979–1986

Hansen coached at OSU for seven seasons, compiling a 107–89 record. His crowning achievement was the 1982–83 season: a 24–7 record, Big 8 Tournament championship, and the school's first NCAA Tournament appearance in 18 years. He was named Big 8 Coach of the Year in 1980–81.

After several inconsistent seasons following the '83 breakthrough, Hansen's contract was not renewed after the 1985–86 season.

Leonard Hamilton

Head Coach 1986–1990

Hamilton's hiring was historic: he became the first African-American head coach in Big 8 Conference history. He came from Kentucky, where he had served as associate head coach under Eddie Sutton.

The early years were a rebuild — his first team went 8–20. But Hamilton was laying groundwork, and his tenure saw the arrival of future NBA players like John Starks and Byron Houston. He compiled a 56–63 record before leaving for Miami.

John Starks

1987–88

Played just one season at OSU, averaging 15.4 points per game in 30 games. Went on to become a New York Knicks legend — one of the most beloved players in franchise history. Doug, he was on your campus.

Byron Houston

1988–1992

Signed with OSU in 1988, arriving at the tail end of Doug's time. Became Oklahoma State's all-time leading scorer, averaging 18.6 points and 9.4 rebounds across 127 games. Three-time First Team All-Big Eight.

Bedlam on the Court

The rivalry with Oklahoma & life in the Big 8

The OU Rivalry

Oklahoma dominated the Big 8 in basketball during the mid-1980s, winning regular season titles in both 1984 and 1985 with 13–1 conference records. The Sooners reached the national championship game in 1988, falling to Kansas. The Bedlam basketball series was intense but tilted heavily toward OU — a reality that made every upset attempt electric inside Gallagher-Iba Arena.

The overall series history stretches back to 1908, with Oklahoma holding the all-time lead at 136–106. But in the House of Horrors, anything could happen.

The Big 8 Landscape

During Doug's years, the Big 8 Conference was a basketball gauntlet. Oklahoma and Kansas were the powers, but Missouri, Iowa State, and Nebraska all had their moments. Oklahoma State's best conference finish during this stretch was the 1982–83 season, when they went 9–5 and finished tied for third.

The conference would evolve into the Big 12 in 1996, adding four Texas schools. But during Doug's time, it was the Big 8 — eight schools, round-robin scheduling, and Saturday afternoons that meant everything.

What Came After Doug Left

The seeds planted during Doug's years bore fruit. Eddie Sutton arrived in 1990 and transformed everything. Two Final Fours. 13 NCAA Tournament appearances. 368 wins. The court named in his honor. The full story of Iba and Sutton — the two coaches Doug mentioned by name — is waiting for you.
