The Soundtrack of Your College Years

Pop Culture

The music, movies, TV, fashion, and tech that defined the 1980s

Music & MTV

The MTV revolution changed everything — and you lived through it

MTV launched on August 1, 1981 — one year before Doug arrived at OSU. By 1982, it had completely transformed how people consumed music. Music videos weren't just promotional tools anymore; they were the art form. And the artists who mastered the medium became the biggest stars on the planet.

Michael Jackson

Thriller • 1982

The biggest-selling artist of the decade. Nine #1 singles. Thriller sold 25 million copies in the '80s alone. The moonwalk. The red jacket. He broke MTV's color barrier.

Prince

Purple Rain • 1984

Along with Jackson, Prince was among the first Black artists in heavy MTV rotation. Purple Rain was both the album and the movie. Genius on every instrument.

The Police

"Every Breath You Take" • 1983

The summer of 1983 belonged to The Police. That song made them the biggest band in the world. Then they broke up.

Bon Jovi

Slippery When Wet • 1986

20+ million copies sold. "Livin' on a Prayer" and "You Give Love a Bad Name." Then New Jersey (1988) sold another 10 million. Arena rock at its peak.

Whitney Houston

Seven Consecutive #1 Hits • 1985–1988

The only artist ever to score seven consecutive #1 songs, from "Saving All My Love for You" to "Where Do Broken Hearts Go."

The British Invasion II

1982–1984

Duran Duran, A Flock of Seagulls, Culture Club, Human League. MTV made them superstars overnight. Synth-pop took over American radio.

The Walkman & the Mixtape

The Sony Walkman (introduced 1979) and the cassette tape were your personal music delivery system. You clipped the Walkman to your belt, popped in a cassette, and created your own private world with foam headphones. And the mixtape? That was how you shared music — and maybe told someone how you felt about them.

Television

What was on the TV in your dorm room

Cheers

Premiered 1982 — Doug's freshman year

Nearly canceled in its first season. Went on to spend eight of eleven seasons in the Nielsen Top 10. The finale drew 93 million viewers. "Where everybody knows your name."

Family Ties

1982–1989

Michael J. Fox as Alex P. Keaton — the young Republican in a liberal household. Fox won three Emmy Awards and became one of the biggest stars of the decade.

The Cosby Show

1984–1992

Dominated the ratings for most of the decade. The Huxtable family became the most-watched family in America. Changed the landscape of network television.

Knight Rider

1982–1986

David Hasselhoff and KITT — the talking, thinking car. The ultimate '80s action show. You wanted that car.

A Different World

1987–1993

Cosby Show spinoff set at fictional Hillman College. As a college student watching a show about college students — it hit different.

Murder, She Wrote

1984–1996

Angela Lansbury as Jessica Fletcher. Twelve seasons. Sunday nights. One of the most successful shows of the era.

MTV Spring Break

Starting in 1986, MTV began annual Spring Break coverage, broadcasting live from Daytona Beach eight hours per day for a week in March. Performers like Mr. Mister and the Beastie Boys played on the beach. It brought spring break culture into millions of living rooms and defined what college spring break was supposed to look like.

Fashion

What people wore on campus in the 1980s

🧥

Members Only Jackets

If you didn't have one, you were a dork. Burgundy was the most popular color, followed by camel brown, grey, and black. The ribbed collar and cuffs were unmistakable.

👔

Preppy Style

The Official Preppy Handbook (1980) launched a movement: polo shirts, khakis, boat shoes, cable-knit sweaters. Ralph Lauren, Brooks Brothers, Lacoste.

👖

Parachute Pants

Slim-cut nylon pants with lots of zippers. Started with breakdancers, went mainstream. Ripstop nylon in every color imaginable.

💇

Big Hair

A defining characteristic of the decade. Extra-hold aerosol hairspray. Side ponies. Feathered bangs. The higher the hair, the closer to God.

👘

Power Suits

Shoulder pads and tailored fits. By 1982, power suits became symbols of strength as women made bold entrances into corporate America.

🥾

Leg Warmers

Flashdance (1983) made them a fashion statement. Worn over jeans, with skirts, at the gym, everywhere. Velour, spandex, and neon colors everywhere.

Tech in the Dorms

No internet. No cell phones. Here's what you actually had.

When Doug arrived at OSU in 1982, the dorms were filled with the click-clack of typewriters at night. By the time he graduated in 1988, that sound had mostly disappeared, replaced by the hum of early personal computers running WordPerfect. But the fundamental experience was analog: no email, no texting, no social media. If you wanted to talk to someone, you walked to their room or called them on the phone.

⌨

Typewriters to Computers

Universities set up rooms with rudimentary computers for writing papers using WordPerfect. By 1988, the typewriter era was ending on college campuses. The Apple II, Commodore 64, and IBM PC were changing everything.

🎧

The Sony Walkman

Your personal music bubble. Pop in a cassette, slip on foam headphones, clip it to your belt. Whether on the bus, at the park, or studying late at night — the Walkman was your constant companion.

📼

Cassette Tapes & Mixtapes

Cassettes powered everything: boomboxes, car stereos, and the sacred art of the mixtape. Recording songs off the radio. Painstakingly labeling each tape. It was the playlist before playlists existed.

📞

Answering Machines

The social media of the '80s. You'd come back to your dorm room and check the blinking light. Missed calls, messages from friends, managing FOMO — all through a little box with a tape inside.

📺

VHS & VCRs

The ability to record TV shows and rent movies was revolutionary. Blockbuster Video opened its first store in 1985. Friday nights meant a trip to the video store.

💻

No Internet

Let that sink in. No email. No Google. No Wikipedia. Research meant going to the library. If you wanted to know something, you looked it up in an encyclopedia or asked someone who knew. Doug wrote his business degree papers and his master's thesis without a single web search.

That Was Your Decade, Doug.

From Michael Jackson to Members Only jackets, from E.T. to Die Hard, from typewriters to early PCs — the 1980s were unlike anything before or since. You lived them in Stillwater, Oklahoma, as a Cowboy. And now you can revisit them anytime.
